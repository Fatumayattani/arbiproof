The shift

The problem
02
A transaction shows what happened. Not whether it was allowed.

Onchain actions are already verifiable, signed and logged. The piece no one can show lives offchain: was the agent authorized to do this, and did the action match what it intended. That is the accountability gap.

02 · The problem
ArbiProof
The idea
03
Bind three things into one proof
01
Authorization
What it was granted
An onchain grant fixes the contract it may call, the recipient it may pay, and a per call cap.
02
Intent
What it declared
Before acting, the agent declares its intent and commits its hash to the receipt.
03
Transaction
What it did
The resulting transaction is bound in, so the action can be checked against the grant.

ArbiProof ties all three into a receipt anyone can audit.

03 · The idea
ArbiProof
How it works
04
Grant to verdict, end to end
Grant
Rules onchain
An authorizer registers what the agent may do: the target, the recipient, the cap.
→
Act
Inside the bound
The agent declares intent and executes. Anything out of scope reverts onchain.
→
Receipt
Anchored proof
It writes a receipt binding the grant, the intent, and the transaction hash.
→
Verify
Recomputed verdict
Anyone loads the receipt and the real transaction and checks it against the grant.

Three parts: contracts that hold the grants and enforce the bounds, an agent that acts and records, and a verifier that judges.

05 · The verdict
ArbiProof
Trust model
06
The agent's word is never trusted.

The verifier loads the real transaction from the chain and recomputes the verdict from scratch. A lying agent cannot fake a pass, because the proof is rebuilt from onchain facts. And out of scope actions revert before a receipt can ever exist.
